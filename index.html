Omoluabi

Editorial intelligence, cultural memory, and civic knowledge.

What this is

Omoluabi is the lead project of Small Systems Lab: a portable device and companion web engine for observing, contextualizing, preserving, and sharing civic information with consent, provenance, accessibility, and human editorial control.

Omoluabi is not a replacement for reporters, archivists, educators, witnesses, or communities. It is infrastructure that helps the person who notices something keep control over how that observation is held, reviewed, translated, preserved, and shared.

Device + web engine

The device

Observe · Contextualize · Preserve · Share. Supports field documentation.

The web engine

Search · Explore Sources · Translate · Timeline · Archive · Settings. Supports review, context, translation, provenance, source discovery, timelines, and preservation.

Rule-based editorial intelligence

Omoluabi works through named states rather than invisible automation. The system may assist with transcription, translation, source discovery, anomaly detection, context mapping, and accessibility formatting, but publication remains human-governed.

  • Human observed
  • Machine suggested
  • Context missing
  • Consent unclear
  • Protected uncertainty
  • Unsafe to publish
  • Ready for review
  • Withheld

Operations layer

Omoluabi must answer operational questions before it publishes:

  • What was observed?
  • Who observed it?
  • What consent exists?
  • What context is missing?
  • Who could be harmed?
  • What evidence supports the claim?
  • What should be withheld?
  • What should be translated?
  • What should be preserved for later review?
  • What is safe to share now?

Ancient geometry as interface logic

Omoluabi uses ancient geometry as a civic interface language.

  • Circle — consent loop, return to community, review cycle.
  • Triangle — witness, source, context.
  • Grid — taxonomy, archive, map, timeline.
  • Spiral — memory, revision, learning over time.
  • Golden ratio — calm proportions for device and web layouts.
  • Threshold — the moment between observe, verify, withhold, and publish.

Accessibility baseline

  • Screen-reader-readable pages.
  • Keyboard-first navigation.
  • High contrast.
  • Captions and transcripts.
  • Alt text for every meaningful image.
  • Plain-language summaries.
  • Translation confidence indicators.
  • Low-bandwidth and offline-first assumptions.
